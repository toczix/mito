## Liver Function

### ALP (Alkaline Phosphatase)

**Optimal Values:**
- Male: 65-100 IU/L
- Female: 65-100 IU/L

**Low ALP:**
- Low Zinc
- Vitamin C and Magnesium have also been associated with low ALP

**High ALP:**
- Biliary dysfunction
- Liver damage
- Bone damage / bone growth (e.g children)
- IBD condition
- Pregnancy

---

### ALT (Alanine Aminotransferase)

**Optimal Values:**
- Male: 13-23 IU/L
- Female: 9-19 IU/L

**Low ALT:**
- Low B6

**High ALT:**
- Liver damage
- Infection (e.g. viral)
- Fatty liver
- Excessive muscle breakdown
- Biliary issues
- Pancreatitis

---

### AST (Aspartate Aminotransferase)

**Optimal Values:**
- Male: 15-25 IU/L
- Female: 12-22 IU/L

**Low AST:**
- Low B6

**High AST:**
- Liver dysfunction
- Infection
- Alcoholism
- Recent exercise
- Hypothyroidism

---

### GGT (Gamma-Glutamyl Transferase)

**Optimal Values:**
- Male: 12-24 IU/L
- Female: 12-24 IU/L (females may safely be 1-2 points lower)

**Low GGT:**
- Low amino acid / glutathione precursors
- Hypothyroidism
- Negative gram bacteria overgrowth

**High GGT:**
- Excess glutathione breakdown
- Toxin exposure (e.g. xenobiotics)
- Biliary dysfunction
- Oxidative stress
- Excess alcohol consumption
- Fatty liver
- Magnesium deficiency

---

### Total Bilirubin

**Optimal Values:**
- Male: 5-13.6 µmol/L (0.29-0.8 mg/dL)
- Female: 5-13.6 µmol/L (0.29-0.8 mg/dL)

**Low Total Bilirubin:**
- Oxidative stress
- Zinc deficiency

**High Total Bilirubin:**
- Increased red blood cell breakdown
- Gilbert's Syndrome
- Bile duct obstruction
- Small intestinal bacterial overgrowth
- Liver dysfunction

---

## Kidney Function

### BUN (Blood Urea Nitrogen)

**Optimal Values:**
- Male: 4.0-6.9 mmol/L (11.2-19.3 mg/dL)
- Female: 4.0-6.9 mmol/L (11.2-19.3 mg/dL)

**Low BUN:**
- Low protein intake / malabsorption
- Excess hydration
- B6 need
- Liver dysfunction

**High BUN:**
- High protein diet
- Dehydration
- Kidney insufficiency
- Catabolism / Adrenal stress
- Dysbiosis (LPS)

---

### Creatinine

**Optimal Values:**
- Male: 60-100 µmol/L (0.68-1.13 mg/dL)
- Female: 60-100 µmol/L (0.68-1.13 mg/dL)

**Low Creatinine:**
- Low protein intake / malabsorption
- Low muscle mass
- Especially common in plant based dieters and elderly people

**High Creatinine:**
- Kidney insufficiency
- Dehydration
- Hyperthyroidism
- Increased muscle mass
- Parasitic infections
- Creatine supplementation will cause a mild increase

---

### eGFR (Estimated Glomerular Filtration Rate)

**Optimal Values:**
- Male: > 90 mL/min/m² (> 60 if high muscle mass)
- Female: > 90 mL/min/m² (> 60 if high muscle mass)

**Low eGFR:**
- Higher than average muscle mass
- Creatine supplementation
- Acute exercise (creatinine will be elevated for 1-3 days post intense training)
- Dehydration
- Poor kidney function / kidney disease

**High eGFR:**
- No reason for concern

---

## Electrolytes

### Bicarbonate (CO2)

**Optimal Values:**
- Male: 25-30 mmol/L
- Female: 25-30 mmol/L

**Low Bicarbonate:**
- Metabolic acidosis
- Respiratory alkalosis (e.g. hyperventilating / shallow breathing)

**High Bicarbonate:**
- Vomiting
- Metabolic alkalosis
- Respiratory acidosis (e.g. inability to properly breathe out CO2)
- Hypochlorhydria (low stomach acid)

---

### Chloride

**Optimal Values:**
- Male: 100-105 mmol/L
- Female: 100-105 mmol/L

**Low Chloride:**
- Vomiting
- Metabolic alkalosis
- Hypochlorhydria (low stomach acid)
- Adrenal insufficiency

**High Chloride:**
- Acidosis
- Hyperventilation (respiratory alkalosis)
- Kidney dysfunction
- Diarrhea
- Dehydration

---

### Potassium

**Optimal Values:**
- Male: 4.0-4.5 mmol/L
- Female: 4.0-4.5 mmol/L

**Low Potassium:**
- Adrenal stress / high aldosterone
- Fluid loss (e.g vomiting, diarrhea, excess sweating)
- High insulin
- Low magnesium

**High Potassium:**
- Dehydration / impaired kidney function
- Acute increase in potassium intake
- Low aldosterone
- Cell damage (e.g. excess exercise)

---

### Sodium

**Optimal Values:**
- Male: 137-143 mmol/L
- Female: 137-143 mmol/L

**Low Sodium:**
- Adrenal insufficiency / low aldosterone
- Low salt intake
- Diuretics
- High blood glucose
- Hypothyroidism
- Addison's disease
- Fluid loss (e.g vomiting, diarrhea, excess sweating)

**High Sodium:**
- Dehydration
- Cushing's disease
- High aldosterone
- High sodium intake

## Minerals

### Calcium

**Optimal Values:**
- Male: 2.3-2.45 mmol/L (9.22-9.8 mg/dL)
- Female: 2.3-2.45 mmol/L (9.22-9.8 mg/dL)

**Low Calcium:**
- Low intake or absorption
- Intestinal damage (e.g. IBD)
- Parathyroid hypofunction
- Vitamin D deficiency
- Low magnesium

**High Calcium:**
- Parathyroid hyperfunction
- Excess vitamin D
- Adrenal hypofunction

---

### Phosphorus

**Optimal Values:**
- Male: 3.0-4.0 mg/dL (0.97-1.29 mmol/L)
- Female: 3.0-4.0 mg/dL (0.97-1.29 mmol/L)

**Low Phosphorus:**
- Poor absorption (e.g. low stomach acid)
- Low vitamin D
- High insulin / refined carbohydrate intake

**High Phosphorus:**
- Excess vitamin D
- Kidney insufficiency
- Parathyroid hypofunction
- Bone growth (e.g. in children)
- Excess processed foods

---

### Serum Magnesium

**Optimal Values:**
- Male: 0.9-1.0 mmol/L (2.19-2.43 mg/dL)
- Female: 0.9-1.0 mmol/L (2.19-2.43 mg/dL)

**Low Serum Magnesium:**
- Adrenal hyperfunction
- Excessive alcohol intake
- Fluid loss (e.g. diarrhea)
- Metabolic dysfunction

**High Serum Magnesium:**
- Hypothyroid
- Dehydration / poor kidney clearance
- Adrenal insufficiency
- Excess breakdown of red blood cells

## Protein

### Albumin

**Optimal Values:**
- Male: 40-50 g/L (4.0-5.0 g/dL)
- Female: 40-50 g/L (4.0-5.0 g/dL)

**Low Albumin:**
- Liver dysfunction
- Kidney dysfunction
- Inflammation / infection
- Pregnancy

**High Albumin:**
- Dehydration

---

### Globulin

**Optimal Values:**
- Male: 22-28 g/L (2.2-2.8 g/dL)
- Female: 22-28 g/L (2.2-2.8 g/dL)

**Low Globulin:**
- Immune insufficiency
- Chronic inflammation / intestinal permeability
- Anemia

**High Globulin:**
- Inflammation / infection / intestinal inflammation
- Autoimmunity
- Gallbladder dysfunction
- Parasites
- Elevated estrogen (e.g. oral contraceptive pill)
- Low serotonin (tryptophan is depleted by elevated globulin)

---

### Total Protein

**Optimal Values:**
- Male: 62-78 g/L (6.2-7.8 g/dL)
- Female: 62-78 g/L (6.2-7.8 g/dL)

**Note:** Because Total Protein does not specify which protein marker is low (Albumin or Globulin), this marker is of little clinical relevance and you should always refer to Albumin and Globulin.

- Male: 4.44-5.0 mmol/L (80-90 mg/dL)
- Female: 4.44-5.0 mmol/L (80-90 mg/dL)

**Low Fasting Glucose:**
- Hypoadrenal function
- Hypothyroidism
- Hypoglycemia
- Nutrient deficiencies

**High Fasting Glucose:**
- Insulin resistance
- Fatty liver
- Pancreatic dysfunction

- Hyperthyroidism
- Pregnancy

---

### Fasting Insulin

**Optimal Values:**
- Male: 13-40 pmol/L (2-6 µIU/mL/mU/L/mIU/L)
- Female: 13-40 pmol/L (2-6 µIU/mL/mU/L/mIU/L)

**Low Fasting Insulin:**
- Type 1 Diabetes
- Pituitary insufficiency
- GABA deficiency
- Low GLP 1

**High Fasting Insulin:**
- Insulin resistance
- Fatty liver
- Pancreatic growths
- Obesity
- Medications
- Pregnancy
- Dysbiosis / LPS

---

### HbA1C (Hemoglobin A1C)

**Optimal Values:**
- Male: 5.0-5.3 % (31-34 mmol/mol)
- Female: 5.0-5.3 % (31-34 mmol/mol)

**Low HbA1C:**
- Hypoglycemia
- Blood loss
- Thalassemia
- Sickle Cell Anemia
- Red blood cells with short lifespan
- Lead toxicity
- Pregnancy

**High HbA1C:**
- Insulin resistance
- Diabetes
- Iron deficiency
- Red blood cells with long lifespan

---

### Triglycerides

**Optimal Values:**
- Male: 0.6-1.0 mmol/L (53-88.5 mg/dL)
- Female: 0.6-1.0 mmol/L (53-88.5 mg/dL)

**Low Triglycerides:**
- Autoimmunity / chronic inflammation
- Low fat intake / vegetarian diet
- Poor fat absorption

**High Triglycerides:**
- Insulin resistance
- Fatty liver
- Hypothyroidism
- Excessive alcohol intake
- Anorexia
- Poor utilisation of fats

## Iron Studies

### Ferritin

**Optimal Values:**
- Male: 50-150 µg/L (50-150 ng/mL)
- Female: 50-150 µg/L (50-150 ng/mL)

**Note:** Personally, I want to see anyone who has a history of iron deficiency to aim for ferritin levels >50, however in the absence of any other iron deficiency patterns or history then ferritin levels between 30-50 may not be indicative of iron deficiency.

**Low Ferritin:**
- Low iron intake / iron deficiency
- Blood loss (e.g internal bleeding, menses)
- Hypochlorhydria / malabsorption
- Chronic infection

**High Ferritin:**
- Hemochromatosis
- Inflammation
- Liver damage / fatty liver
- Anemia of chronic disease
- B6 deficiency

---

### Serum Iron

**Optimal Values:**
- Male: 14.3-23.2 µmol/L (80-130 µg/dL)
- Female: 14.3-23.2 µmol/L (80-130 µg/dL)

**Low Serum Iron:**
- Infection / inflammation
- Blood loss (e.g internal bleeding, menses)
- Liver dysfunction
- Iron deficiency
- Hypochlorhydria

**High Serum Iron:**
- Hemochromatosis
- Vitamin B6 deficiency
- Infection / parasitic infection
- Thalassemia
- Hemolytic anemia

---

### TIBC (Total Iron Binding Capacity)

**Optimal Values:**
- Male: 44-62 µmol/L (250-350 mg/dL)
- Female: 44-62 µmol/L (250-350 mg/dL)

**Low TIBC:**
- Inflammation
- Anemia of chronic disease / infection
- Liver dysfunction
- Iron overload
- Thalassemia

**High TIBC:**
- Iron deficiency anemia
- Pregnancy
- High estrogen / contraceptive pill

---

### Transferrin Saturation %

**Optimal Values:**
- Male: 20-35 %
- Female: 20-35 %

**Low Transferrin Saturation %:**
- Anemia of iron deficiency
- Anemia of chronic disease / infection

**High Transferrin Saturation %:**
- Iron overload
- Hemochromatosis
- Thalassemia
- Iron supplementation / infusion
- Acute inflammation

## Red Blood Cells

### Hemoglobin

**Optimal Values:**
- Male: 145-155 g/L (14.5-15.5 g/dL)
- Female: 135-145 g/L (13.5-14.5 g/dL)

**Low Hemoglobin:**
- Anemia (caused by low iron, B vitamins, copper, magnesium, blood loss/ donating blood / heavy periods, low stomach acid causing malabsorption)
- Pregnancy (due to increased blood volume)

**High Hemoglobin:**
- Dehydration (common causes are low water intake, excess exercise, parasites)
- Asthma / Emphysema (due to impaired oxygen levels, red blood cells increase to try and increase oxygenation)

---

### HCT (Hematocrit)

**Optimal Values:**
- Male: 38-48 %
- Female: 38-48 % (common for women to be slightly lower and men to be slightly higher, but should still fall within this range)

**Low HCT:**
- Anemia (caused by low iron, B12, B9, B6, copper, or blood loss)
- Digestive inflammation (IBD conditions have a strong correlation to low HCT levels)
- Thymus dysfunction

**High HCT:**
- Dehydration
- Respiratory distress (asthma, emphysema)
- Polycythemia
- Smoking
- Testosterone / steroid use
- High altitude

---

### RBC (Red Blood Cell Count)

**Optimal Values:**
- Male: 4.2-4.9 ×10¹²/L
- Female: 3.9-4.5 ×10¹²/L

**Low RBC:**
- Anemia (caused by low iron, B12, B9, B6, copper, or blood loss)
- Pregnancy

**High RBC:**
- Dehydration
- Respiratory distress (asthma, emphysema)
- Polycythemia
- Testosterone / steroid use
- High altitude

---

### MCV (Mean Corpuscular Volume)

**Optimal Values:**
- Male: 82-89 fL
- Female: 82-89 fL

**Low MCV:**
- Anemia (caused by low iron, B6, blood loss, heavy metal toxicity, parasites, or a genetic disorder - Thalassemia)

**High MCV:**
- Anemia (caused by low B12 or B9, low stomach acid, heavy metals, and bacterial overgrowth are also associated)
- Hypothyroidism

---

### MCH (Mean Corpuscular Hemoglobin)

**Optimal Values:**
- Male: 28-32 pg
- Female: 28-32 pg

**Low MCH:**
- Anemia (caused by low iron, B6, blood loss)
- Heavy metal burden
- Vitamin C need
- Thalassemia (a genetic disorder with abnormal hemoglobin)

**High MCH:**
- Anemia (B12/B9)
- Nutrient malabsorption (e.g. hypochlorhydria, celiac disease, gut permeability)
- Excess alcohol intake

---

### MCHC (Mean Corpuscular Hemoglobin Concentration)

**Optimal Values:**
- Male: 32-35 g/dL (320-350 g/L)
- Female: 32-35 g/dL (320-350 g/L)

**Low MCHC:**
- Anemia (caused by low iron, B6, blood loss)
- Heavy metal burden
- Vitamin C need
- Thalassemia (a genetic disorder with abnormal hemoglobin)

**High MCHC:**
- Anemia (B12/B9)
- Nutrient malabsorption (e.g. hypochlorhydria, celiac disease, gut permeability)
- Excess alcohol intake

---

### RDW (Red Cell Distribution Width)

**Optimal Values:**
- Male: < 13 %
- Female: < 13 %

**Low RDW:**
- I am not convinced that low RDW is a concern.

**High RDW:**
- Low MCV + low RBC, low hemoglobin, low iron, low ferritin, low transferrin saturation could suggest iron deficiency anemia

## White Blood Cells

### WBC (White Blood Cell Count)

**Optimal Values:**
- Male: 5.5-7.5 ×10³/µL
- Female: 5.5-7.5 ×10³/µL

**Low WBC:**
- Chronic infection or inflammation (bacteria, viral, or parasitic are most common)
- Autoimmunity
- Decreased production from bone marrow

**High WBC:**
- Acute infection or inflammation (bacterial / viral are most common)
- Diet high in sugar and refined carbohydrates
- Acute stress

---

**Optimal Values:**

- Female: 3.0-4.5 ×10³/µL

**Low Neutrophils:**
- Chronic infection or inflammation (bacteria, viral, or parasitic are most common)
- Decreased production from bone marrow
- Parasites
- Chronic intestinal inflammation (e.g Permeability or IBD)
- Autoimmunity
- Copper deficiency

**High Neutrophils:**
- Acute infection or inflammation (e.g bacterial)
- Asthma

- Pregnancy

---

### Lymphocytes

**Optimal Values:**
- Male: 1.1-3.1 ×10³/µL
- Female: 1.1-3.1 ×10³/µL

**Low Lymphocytes:**
- Chronic infection or inflammation
- Zinc deficiency
- Cancer

**High Lymphocytes:**
- Viral infections
- IBD
- Autoimmunity
- Low cortisol
- Systemic toxicity
- Acute stress / exercise

---

### Monocytes

**Optimal Values:**
- Male: 0.3-0.5 ×10³/µL
- Female: 0.3-0.5 ×10³/µL

**Low Monocytes:**
- May not always be of clinical significance
- Immunosuppressive medications
- Medical conditions that affect the bone marrow (e.g. HIV)
- Chronic immune dysregulation / intestinal permeability

**High Monocytes:**
- Chronic inflammation
- End stage of a viral infection
- Urinary tract congestion (e.g. BPH)
- Parasitic infections
- Non alcoholic fatty liver

---

### Eosinophils

**Optimal Values:**
- Male: 0.0-0.3 ×10³/µL
- Female: 0.0-0.3 ×10³/µL

**Low Eosinophils:**
- Not usually a concern, but may occur due to elevated cortisol

**High Eosinophils:**
- Allergies (e.g food or environmental)
- Asthma
- Low cortisol
- Some parasitic infections, eg worms may cause elevated eosinophils
- Some medication use may cause elevated eosinophils

## Lipids

### Total Cholesterol

**Optimal Values:**
- Male: 4.2-6.4 mmol/L (162-240 mg/dL)
- Female: 4.2-6.4 mmol/L (162-240 mg/dL)

**Note:** Women post menopause could safely have levels around 20% higher than this, and younger people (e.g. <34) may have levels a little lower sometimes.

**Low Total Cholesterol:**
- Liver dysfunction
- Hyperthyroidism
- Low fat intake / fat malabsorption
- Chronic inflammation
- Some studies show association with low steroidal hormones

**High Total Cholesterol:**
- Biliary insufficiency / gallbladder dysfunction
- Hypothyroidism
- High Insulin
- Gene variations
- Pregnancy

---

### HDL Cholesterol

**Optimal Values:**
- Male: 1.29-2.2 mmol/L (50-85 mg/dL)
- Female: 1.29-2.2 mmol/L (50-85 mg/dL)

**Note:** HDL Cholesterol on the higher end of this range seems to be more protective in females than in males.

**Low HDL Cholesterol:**
- Cholestasis (low bile flow)
- Insulin resistance / obesity
- Hyperthyroidism
- Anabolic steroid use

**High HDL Cholesterol:**
- Negative gram bacteria overgrowth (high LPS)
- Intestinal permeability
- Liver dysfunction
- Excessive alcohol intake
- Inflammation / autoimmunity

---

### LDL Cholesterol

**Optimal Values:**
- Male: 2.07-4.4 mmol/L (80-170 mg/dL)
- Female: 2.07-4.4 mmol/L (80-170 mg/dL)

**Note:** Women post menopause could safely have levels around 20% higher than this, and younger people (e.g. <34) may have levels a little lower sometimes.

**Low LDL Cholesterol:**
- Inflammation / LPS
- Hyperthyroidism
- Low fat intake / fat malabsorption

**High LDL Cholesterol:**
- Biliary insufficiency / gallbladder dysfunction
- Hypothyroidism
- High Insulin
- Gene variations
- Pregnancy
- Anabolic steroid use

## Thyroid

### TSH (Thyroid Stimulating Hormone)

**Optimal Values:**
- Male: 1.0-2.5 mIU/L
- Female: 1.0-2.5 mIU/L

**Note:** I would class an 'acceptable' range in the absence of other symptoms or thyroid dysfunction as 0.5-4.5 mIU/L. This is based on large amounts of within-person variability depending on time of day, acute stress, temperature, and season.

**Low TSH:**
- Primary Hyperthyroidism (e.g. Grave's Disease) (thyroid gland dysfunction)
- Secondary Hypothyroidism (pituitary gland dysfunction)
- Tertiary Hypothyroidism (hypothalamus dysfunction)
- Dopamine agonists
- Recent COVID infection
- Exogenous Thyroid Hormone use
- Pregnancy
- HPA axis dysfunction

**High TSH:**
- Primary Hypothyroidism (e.g. Hashimoto's) (thyroid gland dysfunction)
- Lithium or fluoride exposure
- Excess iodine supplementation
- Older age (older individuals may have high TSH levels without hypothyroidism)
- Acute illness may cause an elevation in TSH

---

- Hypothyroidism (secondary)

- Malnourishment / nutrient deficiencies
- Chronic inflammation / infection

**High Free T3:**

- Pregnancy
- Excess iodine intake

## Vitamins

### Vitamin B12

**Optimal Values:**
- Male: 350-650 pmol/L (474-880 pg/mL)
- Female: 350-650 pmol/L (474-880 pg/mL)

**Low Vitamin B12:**
- Low animal protein intake
- Low stomach acid / gastritis
- Megaloblastic anemia
- SIBO
- Dementia
- Metformin or PPI use
- Hypothyroidism

**High Vitamin B12:**
- Excess supplementation
- Functional deficiency (may exhibit deficiency symptoms)
- Liver or kidney dysfunction
- High neutrophil activity
- Cancer

---

### Vitamin D (25-Hydroxy D)

**Optimal Values:**
- Male: 125-225 nmol/L (50-90 ng/mL)
- Female: 125-225 nmol/L (50-90 ng/mL)

**Low Vitamin D:**
- Vitamin D deficiency
- Correlated with cardiovascular disease, metabolic diseases, and cancer
- Osteoporosis
- Inflammation or infection
- Autoimmunity
- Genetic mutations (affecting conversion or receptors)
- Medication use (e.g. cholestyramine)

**High Vitamin D:**
- Excess supplementation
- Excess calcium and kidney stone formation
- Magnesium deficiency

---

### Serum Folate

**Optimal Values:**
- Male: 34-59 nmol/L (15-26 ng/mL)
- Female: 34-59 nmol/L (15-26 ng/mL)

**Low Serum Folate:**
- Low dietary folate intake
- Megaloblastic anemia
- Neural tube defect
- Medication use (e.g. Metformin, oral contraception)

**High Serum Folate:**
- Excess supplementation
- Maternal risks
- May be associated with bacterial overgrowth
