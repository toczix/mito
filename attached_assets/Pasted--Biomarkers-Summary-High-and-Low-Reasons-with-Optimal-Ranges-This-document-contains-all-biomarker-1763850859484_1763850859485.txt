# Biomarkers Summary: High and Low Reasons with Optimal Ranges

This document contains all biomarkers with their associated reasons for high and low values, along with optimal ranges, extracted from the biomarkersdata folder.

---

## Complete Blood Count

### Hemoglobin

**Optimal Values:**
- Females: 135-145g/L or 13.5-14.5 g/dl
- Males: 145-155g/L or 14.5-15.5 g/dl

**Low Hemoglobin:**

- Anemia (caused by low iron, B vitamins, copper, magnesium, blood loss/ donating blood / heavy periods, low stomach acid causing malabsorption)

- Pregnancy (due to increased blood volume)

**High Hemoglobin:**

- Dehydration (common causes are low water intake, excess exercise, parasites)

- Asthma / Emphysema (due to impaired oxygen levels, red blood cells increase to try and increase oxygenation)

---

### MCV (Mean Corpuscular Volume)

**Optimal Values:**
- 82-89fL

**Low MCV:**

- Anemia (caused by low iron, B6, blood loss, heavy metal toxicity, parasites, or a genetic disorder - Thalassemia)

**High MCV:**

- Anemia (caused by low B12 or B9, low stomach acid, heavy metals, and bacterial overgrowth are also associated)

- Hypothyroidism

---

### RDW (Red Cell Distribution Width)

**Optimal Values:**
- <13%

**Low RDW:**

- I am not convinced that low RDW is a concern.

**High RDW:**

- Low MCV + low RBC, low hemoglobin, low iron, low ferritin, low transferrin saturation could suggest iron deficiency anemia

---

### HCT (Hematocrit)

**Optimal Values:**
- 38-48% - it is common for women to be slightly lower and men to be slightly higher, but should still fall within this range

**Low HCT:**

- Anemia (caused by low iron, B12, B9, B6, copper, or blood loss)

- Digestive inflammation (IBD conditions have a strong correlation to low HCT levels)

- Thymus dysfunction

**High HCT:**

- Dehydration

- Respiratory distress (asthma, emphysema)

- Polycythemia

- Smoking

- Testosterone / steroid use

- High altitude

---

### RBC (Red Blood Cells)

**Optimal Values:**
- 3.9-4.5 x10E12/L (females)
- 4.2-4.9 x10E12/L (males)

**Low RBC:**

- Anemia (caused by low iron, B12, B9, B6, copper, or blood loss)

- Pregnancy

**High RBC:**

- Dehydration

- Respiratory distress (asthma, emphysema)

- Polycythemia

- Testosterone / steroid use

- High altitude

---

### MCH (Mean Corpuscular Hemoglobin)

**Optimal Values:**
- 28-32pg

**Low MCH:**

- Anemia (caused by low iron, B6, blood loss)

- Heavy metal burden

- Vitamin C need

- Thalassemia (a genetic disorder with abnormal hemoglobin)

**High MCH:**

- Anemia (B12/B9)

- Nutrient malabsorption (e.g. hypochlorhydria, celiac disease, gut permeability)

- Excess alcohol intake

---

### MCHC (Mean Corpuscular Hemoglobin Concentration)

**Optimal Values:**
- 32-35g/dl OR 320-350g/l

**Low MCHC:**

- Anemia (caused by low iron, B6, blood loss)

- Heavy metal burden

- Vitamin C need

- Thalassemia (a genetic disorder with abnormal hemoglobin)

**High MCHC:**

- Anemia (B12/B9)

- Nutrient malabsorption (e.g. hypochlorhydria, celiac disease, gut permeability)

- Excess alcohol intake

---

### WBC (White Blood Cell Count)

**Optimal Values:**
- 5.5-7.5 x10e3/ul

**Low WBC:**

- Chronic infection or inflammation (bacteria, viral, or parasitic are most common)

- Autoimmunity

- Decreased production from bone marrow

**High WBC:**

- Acute infection or inflammation (bacterial / viral are most common)

- Diet high in sugar and refined carbohydrates

- Acute stress

---

### Neutrophils

**Optimal Values:**
- 3.0-4.5 x10e3/ul

**Low Neutrophils:**

- Chronic infection or inflammation (bacteria, viral, or parasitic are most common)

- Decreased production from bone marrow

- Parasites

- Chronic intestinal inflammation (e.g Permeability or IBD)

- Autoimmunity

- Copper deficiency

**High Neutrophils:**

- Acute infection or inflammation (e.g bacterial)

- Asthma

- Acute stress

- Pregnancy

---

### Lymphocytes

**Optimal Values:**
- 1.1-3.1 x10e3/ul

**Low Lymphocytes:**

- Chronic infection or inflammation

- Zinc deficiency

- Cancer

**High Lymphocytes:**

- Viral infections

- IBD

- Autoimmunity

- Low cortisol

- Systemic toxicity

- Acute stress / exercise

---

### Monocytes

**Optimal Values:**
- 0.3-0.5 x10e3/ul

**Low Monocytes:**

- May not always be of clinical significance

- Immunosuppressive medications

- Medical conditions that affect the bone marrow (e.g. HIV)

- Chronic immune dysregulation / intestinal permeability

**High Monocytes:**

- Chronic inflammation

- End stage of a viral infection

- Urinary tract congestion (e.g. BPH)

- Parasitic infections

- Non alcoholic fatty liver

---

### Eosinophils

**Optimal Values:**
- 0.0-0.3 x10e3/ul

**Low Eosinophils:**

- Not usually a concern, but may occur due to elevated cortisol

**High Eosinophils:**

- Allergies (e.g food or environmental)

- Asthma

- Low cortisol

- Some parasitic infections, eg worms may cause elevated eosinophils

- Some medication use may cause elevated eosinophils

---

### Basophils

**Optimal Values:**
- 0.0 x10e3/ul
- (Note: some labs have a higher sensitivity for reporting basophils, while most will round up or down (e.g. either 0.0 OR 0.1), select labs will provide a specific result (e.g. 0.06). Anything up to around 0.09 appears to be optimal).

**Low Basophils:**

- Not usually a concern

- Pregnancy

- Hyperthyroidism

- Corticosteroid medications

**High Basophils:**

- Allergies

- Inflammation

- Intestinal permeability

- Thyroid hypofunction

- Parasitic infections

- Chronic hemolytic anemia

- Influenza

---

### Platelets

**Optimal Values:**
- 200-300 x10e3/ul

**Low Platelets:**

- Alcoholism

- Liver dysfunction

- Infections

- Bleeding

- Heavy metals

**High Platelets:**

- Iron deficiency

- Hemolytic anemia

- Stress

- Infection

- Inflammation

- Atherosclerosis

- Cancer

## Metabolic Markers

### Sodium

**Optimal Values:**
- 137-143mmol/L

**Low Sodium:**

- Adrenal insufficiency / low aldosterone

- Low salt intake

- Diuretics

- High blood glucose

- Hypothyroidism

- Addison's disease

- Fluid loss (e.g vomiting, diarrhea, excess sweating)

**High Sodium:**

- Dehydration

- Cushing's disease

- High aldosterone

- High sodium intake

---

### Potassium

**Optimal Values:**
- 4.0-4.5mmol/L

**Low Potassium:**

- Adrenal stress / high aldosterone

- Fluid loss (e.g vomiting, diarrhea, excess sweating)

- High insulin

- Low magnesium

**High Potassium:**

- Dehydration / impaired kidney function

- Acute increase in potassium intake

- Low aldosterone

- Cell damage (e.g. excess exercise)

---

### Chloride

**Optimal Values:**
- 100-105mmol/L

**Low Chloride:**

- Vomiting

- Metabolic alkalosis

- Hypochlorhydria (low stomach acid)

- Adrenal insufficiency

**High Chloride:**

- Acidosis

- Hyperventilation (respiratory alkalosis)

- Kidney dysfunction

- Diarrhea

- Dehydration

---

### Bicarbonate / CO2

**Optimal Values:**
- 25-30mmol/L

**Low Bicarbonate:**

- Metabolic acidosis

- Respiratory alkalosis (e.g. hyperventilating / shallow breathing)

**High Bicarbonate:**

- Vomiting

- Metabolic alkalosis

- Respiratory acidosis (e.g. inability to properly breathe out CO2)

- Hypochlorhydria (low stomach acid)

---

### BUN (Blood Urea Nitrogen)

**Optimal Values:**
- 4.0-6.9mmol/L or 11.2-19.3mg/d

**Low BUN:**

- Low protein intake / malabsorption

- Excess hydration

- B6 need

- Liver dysfunction

**High BUN:**

- High protein diet

- Dehydration

- Kidney insufficiency

- Catabolism / Adrenal stress

- Dysbiosis (LPS)

---

### Creatinine

**Optimal Values:**
- 60-100umol/L or 0.68-1.13mg/dl

**Low Creatinine:**

- Low protein intake / malabsorption

- Low muscle mass

- Low creatinine is especially common in plant based dieters and elderly people

**High Creatinine:**

- Kidney insufficiency

- Dehydration

- Hyperthyroidism

- Increased muscle mass

- Parasitic infections

- Creatine supplementation will cause a mild increase

---

### Calcium

**Optimal Values:**
- 2.3-2.45mmol/L or 9.22-9.8mg/dl

**Low Calcium:**

- Low intake or absorption

- Intestinal damage (e.g. IBD)

- Parathyroid hypofunction

- Vitamin D deficiency

- Low magnesium

**High Calcium:**

- Parathyroid hyperfunction

- Excess vitamin D

- Adrenal hypofunction

---

### Phosphorus

**Optimal Values:**
- 3.0-4.0mg/dl OR 0.97-1.29mmol/l

**Low Phosphorus:**

- Poor absorption (e.g. low stomach acid)

- Low vitamin D

- High insulin / refined carbohydrate intake

**High Phosphorus:**

- Excess vitamin D

- Kidney insufficiency

- Parathyroid hypofunction

- Bone growth (e.g. in children)

- Excess processed foods

---

### Total Protein

**Optimal Values:**
- 62-78g/L or 6.2-7.8g/dL

**High or Low Total Protein:**

- Whether Total Protein is High, Low, or Normal, we must always assess Albumin and Globulin levels. Traditionally people claim that low Total Protein can be a sign of malnourishment, under the 'Albumin' and 'Globulin' sections I talk about this more, however I personally wouldn't use Total Protein as a strong indicator of malnourishment.

---

### Albumin

**Optimal Values:**
- 40-50g/L or 4.0-5.0g/dL

**Low Albumin:**

- Liver dysfunction

- Kidney dysfunction

- Inflammation / infection

- Pregnancy

**High Albumin:**

- Dehydration

---

### Globulin

**Optimal Values:**
- 22-28g/L or 2.2-2.8g/dL

**Low Globulin:**

- Immune insufficiency

- Chronic inflammation / intestinal permeability

- Anemia

**High Globulin:**

- Inflammation / infection / intestinal inflammation

- Autoimmunity

- Gallbladder dysfunction

- Parasites

- Elevated estrogen (e.g. oral contraceptive pill)

- Low serotonin (tryptophan is depleted by elevated globulin)

**High LDH:**

**Low Total Bilirubin:**

**High Total Bilirubin:**

### GGT (Gamma Glutamyl Transferase)

**Optimal Values:**
- 12-24IU/L
- (Note: Some studies assessing mortality risk suggest females may safely be 1-2 points lower than this.)

**Low GGT:**

- Low amino acid / glutathione precursors

- Hypothyroidism

- Negative gram bacteria overgrowth (while this isn't established in the literature, this is a pattern I have seen in many clients)

**High GGT:**

- Excess glutathione breakdown

- Toxin exposure (e.g. xenobiotics)

- Biliary dysfunction

- Oxidative stress

- Excess alcohol consumption

- Fatty liver

- Magnesium deficiency

## Lipid Panel

### Total Cholesterol

**Optimal Values:**
- 4.2-6.4mmol/L or 162-240mg/dL
- (Note: Women post menopause could safely have levels around 20% higher than this, and younger people (e.g. <34) may have levels a little lower sometimes.)

**Low Total Cholesterol:**

- Liver dysfunction

- Hyperthyroidism

- Low fat intake / fat malabsorption

- Chronic inflammation

- Some studies show association with low steroidal hormones

**High Total Cholesterol:**

- Biliary insufficiency / gallbladder dysfunction

- Hypothyroidism

- High Insulin

- Gene variations

- Pregnancy

---

### HDL Cholesterol

**Optimal Values:**
- 1.29-2.2mmol/L or 50-85mg/dL
- (Note: HDL Cholesterol on the higher end of this range seems to be more protective in females than in males.)

**Low HDL Cholesterol:**

- Cholestasis (low bile flow)

- Insulin resistance / obesity

- Hyperthyroidism

- Anabolic steroid use

**High HDL Cholesterol:**

- Negative gram bacteria overgrowth (high LPS)

- Intestinal permeability

- Liver dysfunction

- Excessive alcohol intake

- Inflammation / autoimmunity

---

### LDL Cholesterol

**Optimal Values:**
- 2.07-4.4mmol/L or 80-170mg/dL
- (Note: Women post menopause could safely have levels around 20% higher than this, and younger people (e.g. <34) may have levels a little lower sometimes.)

**Low LDL Cholesterol:**

- Inflammation / LPS

- Hyperthyroidism

- Low fat intake / fat malabsorption

**High LDL Cholesterol:**

- Biliary insufficiency / gallbladder dysfunction

- Hypothyroidism

- High Insulin

- Gene variations

## Iron Panel

### Serum Iron

**Optimal Values:**
- 14.3-23.2umol/L or 80-130mcg/dL

**Low Serum Iron:**

- Infection / inflammation

- Blood loss (e.g internal bleeding, menses)

- Liver dysfunction

- Iron deficiency

- Hypochlorhydria

**High Serum Iron:**

- Hemochromatosis

- Vitamin B6 deficiency

- Infection / parasitic infection

- Thalassemia

- Hemolytic anemia

---

### Ferritin

**Optimal Values:**
- 50-150ug/L or 50-150ng/ml
- (Note: Personally, I want to see anyone who has a history of iron deficiency to aim for ferritin levels >50, however in the absence of any other iron deficiency patterns or history then ferritin levels between 30-50 may not be indicative of iron deficiency.)

**Low Ferritin:**

- Low iron intake / iron deficiency

- Blood loss (e.g internal bleeding, menses)

- Hypochlorhydria / malabsorption

- Chronic infection

**High Ferritin:**

- Hemochromatosis

- Inflammation

- Liver damage / fatty liver

- Anemia of chronic disease

- B6 deficiency

---

**Low TIBC:**

**High TIBC:**

## Thyroid Markers

### TSH (Thyroid Stimulating Hormone)

**Optimal Values:**
- 1.0-2.5mIU/L
- (Note: I would class an 'acceptable' range in the absence of other symptoms or thyroid dysfunction as 0.5-4.5mIU/L. This is based on large amounts of within-person variability depending on time of day, acute stress, temperature, and season. This is why several studies suggest that majority of abnormal TSH readings normalise within a few months by themselves.)

**Low TSH:**

- Primary Hyperthyroidism (e.g. Grave's Disease) (thyroid gland dysfunction)

- Secondary Hypothyroidism (pituitary gland dysfunction)

- Tertiary Hypothyroidism (hypothalamus dysfunction)

- Dopamine agonists

- Recent COVID infection

- Exogenous Thyroid Hormone use

- Pregnancy

- HPA axis dysfunction

**High TSH:**

- Primary Hypothyroidism (e.g. Hashimoto's) (thyroid gland dysfunction)

- Lithium or fluoride exposure

- Excess iodine supplementation

- Older age (older individuals may have high TSH levels without hypothyroidism)

- Acute illness may cause an elevation in TSH

---

### Free T4

**Optimal Values:**
- 1.0-1.55ng/dl OR 13.0-20.0pmol/L

**Low Free T4:**

- Hypothyroidism (primary)

- Hypothyroidism (secondary)

- Hypothyroidism (tertiary)

- Malnourishment

**High Free T4:**

- Hyperthyroidism

- Thyroid medication

- Pregnancy

---

### Free T3

**Optimal Values:**
- 3.0-4.5pg/ml OR 4.6-6.9pmol/L

**Low Free T3:**

- Hypothyroidism (primary)

- Hypothyroidism (secondary)

- Hypothyroidism (tertiary)

- Malnourishment / nutrient deficiencies

- Chronic inflammation / infection

- Associated with high cholesterol

**High Free T3:**

- Hyperthyroidism

- Thyroid medication

- Pregnancy

- Excess iodine intake

---

### eGFR (Estimated Glomerular Filtration Rate)

**Optimal Values:**
- Conventionally the target value is >90mL/min/m2. However, in those with higher creatinine levels due to muscle mass, a lower score is to be expected. Generally I would expect most healthy individuals to have an eGFR of >60mL/min/m2

**Low eGFR:**

- Higher than average muscle mass

- Creatine supplementation

- Acute exercise (creatinine will be elevated for 1-3 days post intense training)

- Dehydration

- Poor kidney function / kidney disease

**High eGFR:**

- No reason for concern

---

### TPO (Thyroid Peroxidase) Antibodies

**Optimal Values:**
- Different labs measure TPO antibodies differently, therefore refer to the lab specific reference range.

**Low TPO Antibodies:**

- No clinical significance (a low test result is desirable)

**High TPO Antibodies:**

- Hashimoto's Thyroiditis (autoimmune hypothyroidism)

- Grave's disease

- Associated with other autoimmune conditions such as Celiac disease and Autoimmune Gastritis

- Associated with spontaneous miscarriage and recurrent miscarriage

---

### Thyroglobulin Antibodies

**Optimal Values:**
- Different labs measure Thyroglobulin antibodies differently, therefore refer to the lab specific reference range.

**Low Thyroglobulin Antibodies:**

- No clinical significance (a low test result is desirable)

**High Thyroglobulin Antibodies:**

- Hashimoto's Thyroiditis (autoimmune hypothyroidism)

- Grave's disease (autoimmune hyperthyroidism)

- Excess iodine intake may be associated with further increasing Thyroglobulin antibodies

---

### Vitamin D (25 Hydroxy D)

**Optimal Values:**
- 125-225nmol/L or 50-90ng/ml

**Low Vitamin D:**

- Vitamin D deficiency

- Correlated with cardiovascular disease, metabolic diseases, and cancer

- Osteoporosis

- Inflammation or infection

- Autoimmunity

- Genetic mutations (affecting conversion or receptors)

- Medication use (e.g. cholestyramine)

**High Vitamin D:**

- Excess supplementation

- Excess calcium and kidney stone formation

- Magnesium deficiency

---

### Homocysteine

**Optimal Values:**
- 6-10umol/L

**Low Homocysteine:**

- Glutathione need

- Low protein intake

- Pregnancy

- Insulin resistance

- Oxidative stress

- Hyperthyroidism

- Excessive folate supplementation

**High Homocysteine:**

- B12/B6/B9 deficiency

- Vitamin C deficiency

- MTHFR gene mutation

- Smoking

- Hypothyroidism

- High muscle mass

- Cardiovascular disease / stroke risk

---

### Sex Hormone Binding Globulin (SHBG)

**Optimal Values:**
- 50-80nmol/L (female)
- 40-50nmol/L (male)

**Low SHBG:**

- PCOS

- Metabolic dysfunction

- Higher bioavailable testosterone

- Hypothyroidism

**High SHBG:**

- Low bioavailable testosterone

- Ageing

- High estrogen / synthetic estrogens (e.g. contraceptives)

- Disordered eating

- Hyperthyroidism

## B Vitamins

### Vitamin B12

**Optimal Values:**
- 350-650pmol/L
- 474-880pg/ml

**Low Vitamin B12:**

- Low animal protein intake

- Low stomach acid / gastritis

- Megaloblastic anemia

- SIBO

- Dementia

- Metformin or PPI use

- Hypothyroidism

**High Vitamin B12:**

- Excess supplementation

- Functional deficiency (may exhibit deficiency symptoms)

- Liver or kidney dysfunction

- High neutrophil activity

- Cancer

---

### Serum Folate

**Optimal Values:**
- 34.09nmol/L - 59nmol/L
- 15ng/ml - 25.96ng/ml

**Low Serum Folate:**

- Low dietary folate intake

- Megaloblastic anemia

- Neural tube defect

- Medication use (e.g. Metformin, oral contraception)

**High Serum Folate:**

- Excess supplementation

- Maternal risks

- May be associated with bacterial overgrowth
